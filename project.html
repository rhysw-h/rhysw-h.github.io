Soccernomics: The Economics of the Football World Cup

This project examines the relationship between football and economics, focusing on the FIFA World Cup. It aims to explore the potential benefits of hosting the tournament by analysing data on tourism arrivals, tourism expenditure, and GDP growth in host nations. The project also looks at the economic impact of sponsorship on corporate stock prices and investigates whether there is any correlation between a country’s GDP per capita and its performance in the tournament. Using a range of data visualisations, including maps and charts, this work aims to provide a deeper understanding of the World Cup’s economic significance and global influence.

Is hosting the World Cup beneficial?

I looked at whether there was an increase in tourism arrivals during the years of the world cup in countries that have hosted it.

I then looked at whether there was an increase in tourism expenditure during the years of the world cup in the same countries.

This chart shows the GDP growth for World Cup winners and host countries. This was to see whether there was a benefit to winning the World Cup compared to hosting it.

Conclusion 1

I observed a positive trend in tourism arrivals in the years following the World Cup, suggesting that more tourists may have visited the host country during the event and continued to do so afterwards. Every host country in our analysis, with the exception of Russia, experienced an increase in tourist arrivals. Russia's deviation from this trend is likely due to COVID-19, which occured a year after that World Cup and reduced global travel drastically. Additionally, we noted an increase in tourism expenditure in three out of the five host countries examined. However, the increase was not substantial enough to attribute solely to the impact of hosting the World Cup. Lastly, I compared GDP growth of hosts and winners in the three World Cup years with complete data for hosts and winners of those respective tournaments. This was to see whether there was a larger benefit to winning the world cup over hosting it, and I found that host countries experienced higher GDP growth than the winners for the most part. It is unclear whether this higher growth was from hosting the World Cup or other endogenous factors that may have occurred during those years.

World Cup Sponsors

The 2022 World Cup in Qatar had 4 main sponsors: Adidas, Hyundai, Coca-cola, and Visa. I created a chart of their stock prices before, during and after the world cup to see whether their values went up due to the sponsorship (the period of the World Cup is shaded in grey). I rebased each one on 100 to make comparison easier. I opted against using an updating API and instead downloaded the API data as the World Cup occurred a while ago and recent data would not be useful in comparing whether sponsoring the tournament was beneficial to their stock prices, although I did give the option to look at stock prices two years after the tournament.

National Team Performance

I created a bubble chart of GDP per capita of World Cup participants and compared it to their points per game of all the tournaments they took part in. A bigger bubble means that the team has participated in more competitions. This was created by datascraping a table of all the teams' performances from Wikipedia and dividing points by games played. I then merged this dataset with a GDP per Capita dataset I found on IMF.

There doesn't seem to be much correlation between GDP per Capita and performance in World Cups but we can see that teams that have participated in more competitions have also achieved the most points per game for the most part.

I created two scatter plots showing points per game and participations: the first one containing countries where football is their most popular sport, and the other one where it is not.

The two scatter charts show that countries where football is the most popular sport have a higher mean points per game than those where it is not. This suggests that football popularity is linked to better World Cup performance. Continents with strong football traditions, such as South America and Europe, tend to outperform those from regions where football is less dominant, such as Asia. This regional difference highlights the influence of football's historical development. The R-squared value for countries where football is the dominant sport is 53%, indicating a moderate correlation between World Cup participation and points per game. For countries where football is not the most popular sport, the R-squared value is 22%, likely due to a smaller dataset, as these countries are less frequent World Cup qualifiers.

Final Conclusion

This project explored the economic impacts of the FIFA World Cup, using multiple data visualisations. Hosting the World Cup appears to bring benefits to host nations, particularly through increased tourist arrivals and, to a lesser extent, tourism expenditure. These trends suggest that hosting this tournament can enhance a nation's visibility and attractiveness on the global stage, even in the years following the event. However, the economic benefits are not uniformly significant, and attributing GDP growth solely to the tournament may not be reliable due to other influencing factors. The analysis of sponsor stock prices showed no consistent patterns of financial gain during the tournament period, raising questions about the immediate economic returns for companies investing in World Cup sponsorships, however there may be an argument for long term benefits, as we saw increasing trends in the stock prices for Adidas, Hyundai and Visa during the following 2 year period. On the other hand, examining GDP per capita against team performance revealed little correlation, indicating that football success is influenced more by other factors, such as sporting infrastructure and football popularity, which showed to affect points per game and the number of times participated. Finally, the study of Russia's 2018 World Cup stadiums locations highlighted the strategic placement of venues in economically significant regions, demonstrating how host nations leverage existing infrastructure for logistical convenience and to maximise regional benefits. The World Cup is a complex event that delivers a mix of economic and cultural significance. While host nations can achieve measurable gains in tourism and possibly GDP growth, the broader impacts are often difficult to quantify. Sponsorship and participation bring global exposure but not guaranteed financial returns.
